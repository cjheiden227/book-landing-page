How To Win Your Case

Learn what to do and what not to do from famous cases and humorous examples from presidential debates.

About the Book

Dr. Thomas Curtis has been a medical-legal psychiatrist in the Los Angeles area since 1973. After a decade of private practice and court consultation in child custody cases, Dr. Curtis became an employee’s doctor in the Workers’ Compensation field.

Could you imagine the uproar from his patients if he exposed them in this book? Instead, examples are drawn from famous cases and political contests.

Defend Against the Defense

How much of the overwhelming DNA evidence against O. J. Simpson was considered by the jury? Answer: none. Why? Because the defense discredited the LAPD. You must defend against the defense.

Overstatement Kills

“l’m John Kerry, and l’m reporting for duty.” Did this over-reach lead to Kerry being swiftboated out of a possible presidency?

Winner of the Worst Witness in the World Award - Brett Kavanaugh

Brett scored 10 out of 10 in all 10 areas--defensiveness, insultingness, evasiveness, turning the question around, self-pitying exaggeration, protesting the procedures, repeating absurdities, attempting false sympathy, and creating a false alibi.

Preparation, Preparation, Preparation

Did Ronald Reagan retain his presidency by a perfectly practiced one-liner? "I will not make an issue of age in this campaign. I am not going to exploit for political purposes my opponent's youth and inexperience." Similarly, did Michael Dukakis blow his debate for the presidency by uttering a singular one-line blunder?

These are four of about 60 sections—attorneys, judges, testimony, settlements, injustice—all interspersed with lighter sections such as liars club, categorical de-niers, cover-up downfalls, litigation likables, questionably credible famous witnesses, and so on.

From the Author

I wrote this book to be entertaining to all readers. However, my primary purpose was to educate the layperson how to navigate litigation.

Litigation manuals and guidelines may be abundantly available for lawyers, doctors, accountants and other specialists. However, such sources are scarcely available for the person actively participating in the litigation.

No matter what type of case you have or which side you're on, the concepts of success in litigation apply to all cases.

If you are involved in litigation, you should invest in it by buying this book.

How to Win Your Case is available on Amazon.
